## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Wincham International Limited (UK) instructed Phil Harrison to pay **£9,600 in total fees** — comprising £6,000 in corporate legal fees and £3,600 for preparation of UK company documents — directly to Wincham International Limited's UK Sterling Tide Account at Clearbank Ltd (Sort Code: 04-06-05, Account: 14689723), despite the sale agreement being contracted with **Wincham Spanish Services SL** (the Spanish entity).

This cross-border billing structure has been independently flagged across multiple source documents as constituting **"significant legal irregularities that may violate both British and Spanish law."**

> **Bottom Line:** These fees should be **deducted as a business expense against UK Corporation Tax**, not as a cost of sale on the Spanish side. This is both the safer and financially superior approach.

## QUESTION 1: Was It Legal for Wincham International Limited (UK) to Charge These Fees?

### The Contractual Facts

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Contracting party** | Wincham Spanish Services SL (Spanish entity) |
| **Service jurisdiction** | Spain — contract states fees *"relate solely to work carried out in Spain and under its jurisdiction"* |
| **Payment directed to** | Wincham International Limited — UK entity, Clearbank Ltd Tide Account |
| **Person requesting payment** | Mark Roach, Managing Director, mark@wincham.com |
| **Amount** | £6,000 (legal fees) + £3,600 (document preparation) = **£9,600 total** |

### Legal Analysis: The £6,000 Fee

The Letter of Engagement was signed exclusively between Los Romeros Limited and **Wincham Spanish Services SL**. The £5,000 + VAT (£6,000) fee was for legal facilitation of the Spanish property sale under Spanish jurisdiction.

**The irregularity:** Mark Roach directed Phil Harrison to pay to Wincham **International** Limited's UK bank account — a completely separate legal entity. The contract identified a Spanish entity; the money went to a UK entity.

This creates actionable legal exposure under:
- **Unjust enrichment** — a UK entity received funds it was not contractually entitled to
- **Fraudulent misrepresentation** — the contract identified a Spanish entity but a UK entity collected the money
- **Section 6.3 exception** — the Letter of Engagement's waiver clauses explicitly preserve claims for *"fraudulent misrepresentation and/or fraudulent concealment prior to the execution of this Letter"*

### Legal Analysis: The £3,600 Fee

The agreement with Wincham Spanish Services SL **expressly excluded** costs for preparing UK company documents for the Spanish Notary, noting:
- This required an **additional £3,000 + VAT (£3,600)**
- The contract stated *"fees for work undertaken in the United Kingdom would be covered by a separate agreement"*

**The irregularity:** No separate UK agreement was produced. Instead, this £3,600 was **bundled with the £6,000 Spanish fee** and paid as a single £9,600 transfer to the same UK Tide account — with no separate UK contract.

### Verdict on Question 1

> ⚠️ **The billing structure is legally irregular and highly actionable on two fronts:**
>
> **Avenue 1 — UK Courts:** A UK entity (Wincham International Limited) received £9,600 into a UK account for services contracted under Spanish law with a Spanish entity. A UK court has jurisdiction over a claim for **unjust enrichment or fraudulent misrepresentation** against Wincham International Limited directly.
>
> **Avenue 2 — Spanish Courts:** The mis-routing of fees via the UK entity may constitute fraudulent misrepresentation under contract Section 6.3, and may violate Spanish consumer protection law (Real Decreto Legislativo 1/2007) given the documented fee markup vs. market comparables.
>
> **Whether definitively "illegal" is for a court to determine — but the structure is unambiguously irregular, potentially fraudulent, and legally actionable in both jurisdictions.**

## QUESTION 2: Spanish CGT Deduction vs. UK Corporation Tax?

### Option A: Deduct as Cost of Sale — Spanish IRNR (Capital Gains Tax)

The AEAT allows deduction of *"verifiable costs directly associated with the sale, such as real estate agency commissions and the legal fees of the Spanish solicitor"* from the CGT taxable base (IRNR at 19% flat rate).

**Why this will almost certainly FAIL:**

| Problem | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Invoice issuer** | Wincham **International** Limited — a UK company |
| **Invoice currency** | GBP (not EUR) |
| **Payment jurisdiction** | UK bank account |
| **Classification in Completion Statement** | *"OFF-STATEMENT DISBURSEMENTS (UK JURISDICTION)"* — explicitly outside the Spanish transaction |
| **AEAT standard** | Requires invoices from Spanish-registered entities for Spanish property sale deductions |

The AEAT will see a UK invoice, paid in GBP, to a UK company, classified as a UK disbursement — and **reject it as a deductible cost of a Spanish real estate transfer.**

### Option B: Deduct as Business Expense — UK Corporation Tax

Los Romeros Limited is a UK tax-resident corporate entity, subject to UK Corporation Tax on its worldwide profits. A GBP invoice from Wincham International Limited (a UK company) for corporate legal services and document preparation is a **standard, documentable, deductible business expense** under s.54 CTA 2009.

**Why this WILL succeed:**

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Invoice issuer** | UK company ✅ |
| **Currency** | GBP ✅ |
| **Nature of expense** | Corporate legal services — wholly and exclusively for business ✅ |
| **Evidence** | Mark Roach emails + bank transfer records ✅ |
| **HMRC standard** | Straightforward deductible under s.54 Corporation Tax Act 2009 ✅ |

### Verdict on Question 2

> ✅ **These costs must be applied as a deductible business expense against UK Corporation Tax.**  
> The Spanish AEAT will reject them. HMRC will accept them. This is not a close call.

## QUESTION 3: Where Is the Best Net Financial Benefit?

### The FTCR Mechanics

Under the **UK-Spain Double Taxation Agreement (2014 Convention)**, double taxation is prevented via **Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR)**:
- Spain taxes the gain first at **19% IRNR (flat rate)**
- The 19% paid to AEAT becomes an FTCR credit offsetting UK Corporation Tax on the same gain
- UK Corporation Tax: **19%** (profits < £50,000) or **25%** (profits > £250,000)

### Why Deducting in Spain = Zero Net Benefit

If £9,600 were somehow accepted by AEAT as a Spanish deductible (hypothetically):

| Step | Effect |
|---|---|
| Spanish taxable gain reduced by £9,600 | Spanish IRNR bill falls by £9,600 × 19% = £1,824 |
| FTCR credit also falls by £1,824 | UK Corp Tax rises by £1,824 to fill the gap |
| **Net effect** | **£0** — the saving is immediately cancelled by FTCR erosion |

### Why Deducting in the UK = Real Tax Savings

| Scenario | Tax Saving |
|---|---|
| **UK Corp Tax at 19% (small profits rate)** | £9,600 × 19% = **£1,824 saved** |
| **UK Corp Tax at 25% (main rate)** | £9,600 × 25% = **£2,400 saved** |
| **Spanish IRNR deduction (hypothetical)** | **£0** (FTCR neutralises it entirely) |

### Verdict on Question 3

> 💰 **The best net financial benefit is unequivocally obtained by deducting all £9,600 as a business expense against UK Corporation Tax.**
>
> - Small profits rate (≤ £50k): saves **£1,824**
> - Main rate (≥ £250k): saves **£2,400**
> - Spanish deduction: saves **£0** (FTCR neutralisation) AND risks AEAT audit rejection
